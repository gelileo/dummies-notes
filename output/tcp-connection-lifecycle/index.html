TCP connection lifecycle

The fixed sequence of stages a TCP conversation between two computers passes through: an opening greeting where both sides agree to talk, a middle where data flows and arrivals are confirmed, and a closing goodbye so both sides know the conversation is over.

Built bottom-up from 4 concept(s).

Communication protocol

An agreed set of rules two computers follow when talking, so each one knows what kind of message to expect next and what every message means.

Data packets

Small labeled chunks that a message is chopped into before being sent across a network, each traveling on its own and carrying enough information to be put back in order.

Best-effort delivery

The way the internet moves packets with no promises: it tries to deliver each one, but packets can be lost, duplicated, delayed, or arrive out of order.

Builds on:

  • Data packets — Best-effort delivery describes the fate of individual packets in transit, so without first picturing a message as separate chunks traveling on their own, there is nothing for the losses, duplicates, delays, and reorderings to happen to.

TCP connection lifecycle

The fixed sequence of stages a TCP conversation between two computers passes through: an opening greeting where both sides agree to talk, a middle where data flows and arrivals are confirmed, and a closing goodbye so both sides know the conversation is over.

Builds on:

  • Communication protocol — TCP is one such rule-set, and its connection lifecycle is exactly the scripted order of messages that rule-set demands, so without the idea of computers following shared rules the stages look arbitrary.
  • Data packets — Every stage of a TCP connection, including the opening greeting and the closing goodbye, is carried out by sending individual packets, so the reader must first picture data moving as separate chunks.
  • Best-effort delivery — The handshake, the receipt confirmations, and the careful two-way goodbye exist only because the network itself guarantees nothing, so without this idea the whole lifecycle seems like pointless ceremony.
